**1**
When assessing a pediatric patient with suspected meningitis, which clinical sign is demonstrated by pain or resistance upon extending the knee while the child is lying supine?
A) Brudzinski's sign
B) Kernig's sign
C) Macewen's sign
D) Chvostek's sign
**Rationale:** B — Remember that Kernig's sign is a classic indicator of meningeal irritation, elicited by flexing the hip to 90 degrees and extending the knee. Brudzinski's sign, on the other hand, involves involuntary hip and knee flexion when you flex the patient's neck.

**2**
Which of the following interventions is NOT appropriate for decreasing cerebral edema?
A) Hydrocortisone administration
B) Hyperventilation therapy
C) Dextrose infusion
D) Mannitol infusion
**Rationale:** C — Managing brain edema relies on manipulating osmotic gradients, meaning hypertonic solutions like mannitol are highly effective for pulling fluid out of the brain to reduce intracranial pressure. You want to strictly avoid hypo-osmolar solutions like 5% dextrose, as they will lower serum sodium and actively push water into the brain tissue, worsening the edema.

**3**
Which of the following nerves does NOT provide motor innervation to the muscles of the forearm?
A) Musculocutaneous nerve
B) Radial nerve
C) Median nerve
D) Ulnar nerve
**Rationale:** A — Keep in mind that while the musculocutaneous nerve provides sensory coverage to the lateral forearm, its motor function is strictly limited to the anterior upper arm muscles like the biceps brachii. The actual motor control for the forearm muscles is handled entirely by the radial, median, and ulnar nerves.

**4**
Which nerve plexus is responsible for innervating the skin and musculature of the upper limbs, along with select chest muscles?
A) Cervical plexus
B) Brachial plexus
C) Lumbar plexus
D) Sacral plexus
**Rationale:** B — The brachial plexus is the vital nerve network that exclusively governs both motor and sensory function for the upper extremities and parts of the chest wall. As a quick anatomical review, the cervical plexus targets the back of the head and neck, while the lumbar and sacral plexuses handle the pelvis and lower limbs.


**5**
Which condition is defined as a malformation of the spine where the posterior portion of the lamina of one or more vertebrae fails to fuse?
A) Rachischisis
B) Encephalocele
C) Spina bifida
D) Anencephaly
**Rationale:** C — Spina bifida occurs when the bony vertebral arch fails to completely close over the spinal cord during embryonic development. This structural defect can range from a hidden bony gap (occulta) to a visible protruding sac (cystica).

**6**
What is the clinical term for a patient's inability to carry out normal, coordinated activities despite having intact motor and sensory function?
A) Anhedonia
B) Apraxia
C) Apathy
D) Amnesia
**Rationale:** B — Apraxia is a neurological condition where the brain struggles to execute complete, coordinated movements even though the muscles themselves are perfectly capable. It is important to distinguish this from muscle weakness or primary sensory deficits.

**7**
A patient who sustained a head injury 24 hours ago suddenly develops slurred speech and becomes disoriented to time and place. What is the nurse's priority action?
A) Recheck the patient's neurologic status in 15 minutes
B) Prepare the patient for emergency surgery
C) Continue hourly neurologic assessments
D) Inform the neurosurgeon of the patient's status immediately
**Rationale:** D — Any sudden change in a head injury patient's level of consciousness or neurological baseline strongly suggests rising intracranial pressure or bleeding. You must immediately notify the provider rather than delaying care with further routine assessments.

**8**
What is the primary nursing intervention when a patient begins experiencing a tonic-clonic seizure?
A) Restrain the patient's arms and legs
B) Elevate the head of the bed
C) Insert a tongue blade into the patient's mouth
D) Implement measures to prevent physical injury
**Rationale:** D — During an active seizure, your primary focus is always patient safety, which means clearing the area of hazards and padding their head. Never attempt to restrain a seizing patient or force anything into their mouth, as this can cause severe dental or airway trauma.

**9**
A patient in the emergency department opens their eyes only in response to painful stimuli, speaks inappropriate words, and moves their limbs on command. What is this patient's Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score?
A) 11
B) 10
C) 9
D) 13
**Rationale:** A — Let's break down the GCS: opening eyes to pain scores a 2, using inappropriate words scores a 3, and obeying motor commands scores a top mark of 6. Adding these components together yields a total GCS score of 11.

**10**
What term is used to describe uncoordinated movements of the extremities in a patient who has suffered a traumatic brain injury?
A) Ataxia
B) Apraxia
C) Dystonia
D) Dyskinesia
**Rationale:** A — Ataxia literally translates to "without coordination" and frequently manifests as clumsy, unsteady movements due to central nervous system damage. This is a common finding when head trauma affects the cerebellum or its related motor pathways.

**11**
Which neurological functions would be most directly impaired by an obstruction in the anterior cerebral arteries?
A) Auditory perception
B) Judgment, insight, and reasoning
C) Balance and coordination
D) Visual integration from language comprehension
**Rationale:** B — The anterior cerebral arteries primarily supply blood to the frontal lobes of the brain. Because the frontal lobe is the command center for higher-level cognitive functions, an ischemic event here will directly compromise the patient's judgment, insight, and reasoning abilities.

**12**
According to the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS), which of the following scores is indicative of a coma in an adult patient?
A) 0
B) 5
C) 9
D) 12
**Rationale:** B — A generally accepted clinical rule of thumb is that a GCS score of 8 or less indicates a comatose state often requiring advanced airway management. Always remember that the lowest possible GCS score is 3, making a score of 0 impossible.

**13**
During nerve conduction, what primary ion movement causes the depolarization of the axolemma?
A) Accumulation of Na+ on the outside
B) Equal efflux of Na+ and K+ across the axolemma
C) Rapid influx of Na+ into the cell
D) None of the above
**Rationale:** C — Depolarization is triggered when voltage-gated channels open, allowing a rapid and massive influx of positively charged sodium (Na+) ions into the axon. This sudden shift changes the internal electrical charge from negative to positive, successfully propagating the action potential.


**14**
Which of the following physiological activities is increased by the activation of the sympathetic nervous system?
A) Heart rate
B) Secretion of digestive juices
C) Secretion of saliva
D) All of the above
**Rationale:** A — The sympathetic nervous system drives our "fight or flight" response, which naturally accelerates the heart rate to pump more blood to muscles. Conversely, it actively inhibits non-essential resting functions like digestion and salivation during a crisis.

**15**
Which areas of the brain typically show atrophy on imaging in a patient with Alzheimer's disease?
A) Parietal and temporal lobes
B) Frontal and temporal lobes
C) Frontal and parietal lobes
D) Parietal and occipital lobes
**Rationale:** A — In Alzheimer's disease, the degenerative process heavily targets the parietal and medial temporal lobes. This specific pattern of tissue loss is what leads to the classic memory and cognitive deficits you see in these patients.

**16**
The accumulation of which substance can cross the blood-brain barrier and lead to the severe neurological injury in newborns known as kernicterus?
A) Bile salts
B) Conjugated bilirubin
C) Unconjugated bilirubin
D) Biliverdin
**Rationale:** C — Because unconjugated bilirubin is highly lipid-soluble, it can easily cross the immature blood-brain barrier of a newborn. If levels get too high, it deposits directly into the basal nuclei, causing permanent neurological damage.

**17**
All of the following substances function as neurotransmitters EXCEPT:
A) Dopamine
B) Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)
C) Serotonin
D) Troponin
**Rationale:** D — Dopamine, GABA, and serotonin are all classic chemical messengers in the nervous system, but troponin is completely different. Troponin is a specific protein complex found in cardiac muscle fibers, making it a critical biomarker for myocardial infarctions, not a neurotransmitter.

**18**
Guillain-Barré syndrome is primarily characterized by immune-mediated damage to which of the following structures?
A) Brain parenchyma
B) Peripheral nervous system
C) Spinal cord
D) Thyroid glands
**Rationale:** B — Guillain-Barré syndrome is an acute autoimmune condition where the body mistakenly attacks the myelin sheaths of the peripheral nerves. This demyelination interrupts signal conduction, leading to the rapid-onset ascending muscle weakness classic to the disease.

**19**
How many pairs of spinal nerves are present in the human nervous system?
A) 28 pairs
B) 30 pairs
C) 31 pairs
D) 33 pairs
**Rationale:** C — The human spinal cord gives rise to exactly 31 pairs of spinal nerves. To break it down quickly: you have 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, and 1 coccygeal pair.

**20**
While assessing an unconscious patient brought to the emergency department after a motor vehicle collision, which finding should cause the nurse the most immediate concern?
A) An elevated body temperature
B) An increased blood pressure
C) Bleeding from the ears
D) All of the above
**Rationale:** C — While all abnormal vitals require attention, bleeding from the ears in a trauma patient strongly suggests a basilar skull fracture. This is a critical emergency because it indicates a high risk for rapidly increasing intracranial pressure and fatal brain herniation.

**21**
Why is it especially critical for nurses to carefully assess and validate complaints of pain reported by elderly patients?
A) They typically have an abnormally increased tactile perception.
B) They frequently exaggerate chronic pain symptoms.
C) They generally have a decreased pain threshold.
D) They often experience reduced overall sensory perception.
**Rationale:** D — Normal aging processes often lead to decreased sensory nerve function and diminished perception of physical sensations. Because their baseline sensation is reduced, an elderly patient reporting significant pain might be experiencing a much more severe underlying pathology than their symptoms initially suggest.

**22**
An emergency room nurse is assessing an adult trauma patient following a motor vehicle collision. Which of the following clinical manifestations directly results from sympathetic nervous system stimulation?
A) Enhanced spatial awareness and attention
B) Generalized decrease in physiological functioning
C) Increased salivary gland production
D) A rapid pulse and an increased respiratory rate
**Rationale:** D — Trauma triggers a massive sympathetic "fight or flight" cascade designed for immediate survival. This surge of adrenaline intentionally dilates the bronchioles for better oxygenation and spikes the heart rate to maximize blood flow to critical organs.



**23**
Which of the following statements is the least accurate regarding the nursing management of a patient undergoing a lumbar puncture?
A) The procedure is often used to withdraw cerebrospinal fluid for diagnostic testing.
B) Cerebrospinal fluid specimens must be carefully labeled in their proper collection sequence.
C) Fluids should be heavily forced both before and after the procedure.
D) Post-procedure assessment must include checking movement and sensation in the lower extremities.
**Rationale:** C — While patients are encouraged to increase fluid intake after a lumbar puncture to help replenish cerebrospinal fluid and minimize headache risk, there is absolutely no clinical requirement to force fluids beforehand.

**24**
A patient diagnosed with cerebral thrombosis is preparing for a cerebral angiography. Which nursing intervention is appropriate for this procedure?
A) Warn the patient that they may experience a warm, flushed sensation and a salty taste when the contrast dye is injected.
B) Avoid applying a pressure dressing over the arterial puncture site.
C) Keep the extremity used for the puncture site flexed to minimize bleeding.
D) All of the above.
**Rationale:** A — The contrast dye used in angiography frequently causes a temporary, harmless hot flush and a metallic or salty taste, so preparing the patient for this prevents unnecessary anxiety. Following the procedure, the extremity must actually be kept strictly straight and a pressure dressing applied to prevent severe bleeding or thrombus formation.

**25**
When assessing a patient at risk for neurological decline, which of the following is considered the earliest clinical sign of increasing intracranial pressure (ICP)?
A) Ipsilateral pupillary dilation
B) Contralateral hemiparesis
C) A progression from restlessness to confusion and lethargy
D) A widening pulse pressure
**Rationale:** C — As rising ICP begins to decrease cerebral perfusion, the resulting mild brain hypoxia first manifests as subtle changes in the patient's level of consciousness, like unexplained restlessness or slight confusion. Classic textbook signs like pupillary changes or widening pulse pressure are late and ominous indicators that herniation may be imminent.

**26**
Which of the following statements regarding the pharmacological management of a patient experiencing a cerebrovascular accident (CVA) is considered inappropriate?
A) Osmotic diuretics are administered to decrease cerebral edema.
B) Aspirin is a primary medication used in the acute management of a completed stroke.
C) Corticosteroids can be utilized to reduce cerebral inflammation and edema.
D) Thrombolytic therapy is most effective when administered within three hours of an occlusive CVA.
**Rationale:** B — Aspirin is a fantastic antiplatelet medication used to prevent recurrent ischemic events, but it is dangerous during the acute management of a stroke because it can exacerbate life-threatening bleeding if the stroke is hemorrhagic.

**27**
A nurse is monitoring a trauma patient 8 hours after a severe motor vehicle collision. Which assessment finding most strongly suggests increasing intracranial pressure (ICP)?
A) Blood pressure decreases from 150/90 mmHg to 110/70 mmHg.
B) The patient awakens when aroused, answers appropriately, but falls back asleep immediately.
C) The heart rate increases from 80 to 92 beats per minute.
D) The patient refuses to eat dinner due to sudden anorexia.
**Rationale:** B — The most sensitive and reliable early indicator of increasing ICP is a deteriorating level of consciousness. A patient who cannot maintain wakefulness without continuous stimulation is demonstrating a significant neurological decline that requires your immediate attention.

**28**
Which class of medications is generally considered the safest option for short-term administration to adults requiring assistance with falling asleep?
A) Barbiturates
B) Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
C) Minor tranquilizers
D) Benzodiazepines
**Rationale:** D — Benzodiazepines are often preferred for temporary sleep induction because they possess a high therapeutic index and a wide safety margin. They effectively decrease nervous system arousal and promote sleep with a much lower risk of fatal respiratory depression compared to older drugs like barbiturates.

**29**
A patient arrives in the emergency department with a head injury following a traumatic accident. The patient is currently drowsy, confused, and presents with unequal pupils. Which nursing diagnosis takes immediate priority?
A) Altered cognitive function
B) Altered cerebral tissue perfusion
C) High risk for physical injury
D) Altered sensory perception
**Rationale:** B — Using the ABCs of clinical prioritization, maintaining adequate circulation and cerebral perfusion is your absolute first priority to prevent further, irreversible hypoxic brain damage. Once the brain is being adequately perfused and the patient is stabilized, you can then safely address secondary concerns like safety risks and cognitive confusion.

**30**
A nurse is caring for a patient diagnosed with myasthenia gravis. Which of the following nursing diagnoses is the highest priority requiring immediate intervention?
A) High risk for injury related to muscle weakness
B) Ineffective coping related to the illness
C) Ineffective airway clearance related to muscle weakness
D) Pain related to muscle weakness
**Rationale:** C — Since myasthenia gravis causes progressive weakness of the respiratory muscles, your absolute priority is always ensuring the patient can maintain an open airway and effectively clear secretions. If the patient loses the ability to breathe adequately or cough, it rapidly becomes a life-threatening emergency.

**31**
A patient arrives at the emergency department following a traumatic head injury, and the nurse notes clear fluid draining from the nose. Which of the following tests is the most reliable for confirming if this drainage is cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)?
A) pH testing
B) Testing for Beta2-transferrin
C) Glucose strip testing
D) Testing for chlorides
**Rationale:** B — While we used to rely on glucose testing, it's actually highly unreliable because normal nasal or tear secretions can cause false positives. The gold standard and most reliable test is checking for Beta-2 transferrin, because this specific protein is exclusively found in cerebrospinal fluid.

**32**
Which of the following clinical manifestations is typically considered an early symptom of myasthenia gravis?
A) Dysphagia
B) Fatigue that improves in the evening
C) Ptosis
D) Respiratory distress
**Rationale:** C — In more than half of myasthenia gravis patients, the very first noticeable symptom is localized ocular muscle weakness, most commonly presenting as drooping eyelids (ptosis) or double vision. Remember that fatigue in these patients typically worsens as the day progresses, not improves.

**33**
A patient is admitted following a thrombotic cerebrovascular accident (CVA). Which of the following nursing assessments takes priority during the first 24 hours of admission?
A) Auscultating bowel sounds
B) Reviewing an electroencephalogram
C) Assisting with an electromyogram
D) Assessing pupil size and pupillary response
**Rationale:** D — During the critical first 24 hours post-stroke, monitoring pupil size and reactivity is vital because changes can rapidly indicate rising intracranial pressure or cranial nerve compression. Catching these subtle neurological shifts early allows for immediate life-saving interventions.

**34**
An adult patient with a brain tumor is preparing for a craniotomy. Which class of medications is primarily utilized to prevent or minimize postoperative cerebral edema?
A) Antihypertensives
B) Antihistamines
C) Diuretics
D) Corticosteroids
**Rationale:** D — Glucocorticoids, like dexamethasone, are the frontline choice for brain tumor patients because their potent anti-inflammatory properties significantly reduce swelling and prevent dangerous cerebral edema post-surgery. This targeted action is crucial for protecting the fragile brain tissue inside the rigid cranial vault.

**35**
An adult patient is recovering in the surgical unit following spinal surgery. Which of the following nursing interventions is the most immediate priority?
A) Assessing the feet for distal sensation and circulation
B) Encouraging a high oral fluid intake
C) Log-rolling the patient into a prone position
D) Observing long-term bowel and voiding patterns
**Rationale:** A — Following any spinal procedure, checking the patient's distal pulses, sensation, and movement is your most critical bedside assessment. Any new loss of feeling or circulation in the feet strongly suggests post-operative spinal cord damage or hematoma compression, which requires immediate physician notification.

**36**
A 23-year-old patient is admitted to the emergency department experiencing a new-onset tonic-clonic seizure. The nurse recognizes that the most likely etiology for seizures in this specific age demographic is:
A) Ischemic stroke
B) Severe electrolyte imbalance
C) Traumatic head injury
D) Congenital brain defect
**Rationale:** C — While strokes are the leading culprit in older adults, traumatic head injuries are by far the most common cause of new-onset seizures in young adults. Keep in mind that post-traumatic seizures don't always happen right away and can actually develop months after the initial head injury.

**37**
The nurse is monitoring an unconscious patient on the medical-surgical floor. Which of the following clinical signs is considered the most reliable early indicator of developing hypoxia?
A) Peripheral cyanosis in the extremities
B) An altered respiratory rate and rhythm
C) Sudden systemic hypertension
D) Unexplained restlessness and agitation
**Rationale:** D — The brain is incredibly sensitive to drops in oxygen, meaning subtle neurological changes like restlessness and agitation are almost always your first warning sign of hypoxia, even in unconscious patients. Never wait for late signs like cyanosis to intervene when a patient's brain is starving for oxygen.

**38**
A patient arrives at the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU) after a surgical procedure utilizing spinal anesthesia. To prevent common post-procedural complications, the nurse should immediately place the patient in which position?
A) Flat in a supine position
B) Left lateral decubitus position
C) Prone position with the head turned laterally
D) Knee-chest position
**Rationale:** A — Following spinal anesthesia, patients should remain strictly flat and supine for several hours to equalize cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) pressure. Keeping them flat prevents CSF leakage from the dural puncture site, which is the primary cause of severe, debilitating spinal headaches.


**39**
Which cranial nerve is primarily responsible for the parasympathetic regulation of the heart rate?
A) Cranial nerve VII
B) Cranial nerve VIII
C) Cranial nerve IX
D) Cranial nerve X
**Rationale:** D — Cranial nerve X, the vagus nerve, acts as a major parasympathetic pathway that directly mediates vital homeostatic reflexes, including heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration.

**40**
Which of the following physiological responses is a direct function of the parasympathetic nervous system?
A) Contraction of arrector pili muscles
B) Stimulation of sweat gland secretion
C) Acceleration of the heart rate
D) Constriction of the pupils
**Rationale:** D — Think of the parasympathetic system as your "rest and digest" control, which naturally constricts the pupils to limit light intake during rest. Functions like accelerating the heart rate and stimulating sweat glands are driven by the sympathetic "fight or flight" response.

**41**
Which type of nerve fibers are responsible for carrying sensory impulses from peripheral receptors directly to the central nervous system?
A) Afferent fibers
B) Efferent fibers
C) Synaptic fibers
D) Nodes of Ranvier
**Rationale:** A — Afferent neurons act as the body's dedicated sensory messengers, constantly delivering incoming stimuli from the skin and organs directly to the brain. Remember that efferent neurons do the exact opposite, carrying outgoing motor commands away from the brain to your muscles.

**42**
Which of the following physiological functions is NOT regulated by the vagus nerve?
A) Motor control of tongue movements
B) Gastrointestinal peristalsis
C) Pancreatic enzyme secretion
D) Heart rate regulation
**Rationale:** A — The motor control of the tongue is exclusively managed by the hypoglossal nerve, which is cranial nerve XII. The vagus nerve is incredibly far-reaching, but it governs parasympathetic organ functions like digestion and heart rate, not tongue movement.

**43**
Flapping hand tremors, also known as asterixis, are classically observed in patients experiencing which of the following clinical conditions?
A) Uremic syndrome
B) Chronic renal failure
C) Hepatic encephalopathy
D) All of the above
**Rationale:** D — Asterixis is a classic neurological sign caused by the severe buildup of metabolic toxins during critical organ dysfunction. You will frequently observe these flapping tremors in patients suffering from uremia, end-stage renal failure, and decompensated liver disease.

**44**
The motor symptoms classic to Parkinson's disease are primarily caused by a progressive deficiency in which of the following neurotransmitters?
A) Acetylcholine
B) Dopamine
C) Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)
D) Serotonin
**Rationale:** B — Parkinson's disease involves the progressive degeneration of specific neurons in the brain that are responsible for producing dopamine. As dopamine levels steadily drop, patients lose vital motor control, leading to hallmark symptoms like resting tremors and bradykinesia.

**45**
What is the primary physiological function controlled by the sixth cranial nerve (Abducens)?
A) Movement of the eyeball
B) Gustatory (taste) perception
C) Closure of the epiglottis
D) Motor movement of the tongue
**Rationale:** A — The abducens nerve specifically innervates the lateral rectus muscle of the eye, which is responsible for pulling the eyeball outward (abduction). It works alongside the oculomotor and trochlear nerves to smoothly coordinate all major eye movements.

**46**
Which of the following involuntary physiological processes is primarily driven by the autonomic nervous system?
A) The patellar knee-jerk reflex
B) Somatic pupillary reflexes
C) Peristalsis of the intestinal tract
D) Voluntary blinking of the eye
**Rationale:** C — The autonomic nervous system operates behind the scenes to control involuntary visceral functions, making it entirely responsible for driving gastrointestinal peristalsis. In contrast, deep tendon responses like the knee-jerk reflex are mediated by somatic spinal pathways.

**47**
Which of the following substances does NOT function as a neurotransmitter in the human body?
A) Cortisone
B) Acetylcholine
C) Epinephrine
D) Norepinephrine
**Rationale:** A — Acetylcholine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine are all classic chemical messengers that transmit neurological signals across synapses. Cortisone, however, is a glucocorticoid steroid hormone released by the adrenal cortex to manage systemic stress and inflammation.

**48**
Why is immunosuppressive therapy considered highly effective in the ongoing management of patients with myasthenia gravis?
A) It decreases the production of autoantibodies that damage acetylcholine receptors.
B) It directly increases the synthesis and release of acetylcholine.
C) It actively inhibits the enzymatic breakdown of acetylcholine in the synapse.

**Rationale:** A — Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder where the body mistakenly attacks its own neuromuscular junctions. Immunosuppressants directly target this root cause by dampening the overall immune response, which halts the production of those destructive autoantibodies.


**49**
Which assessment is specifically required to ensure the safe administration of intravenous mannitol?
A) Assessing vital signs every four hours
B) Obtaining a daily weight
C) Monitoring hourly urine output
D) Checking the level of consciousness every eight hours
**Rationale:** C — Mannitol is a potent osmotic diuretic that rapidly pulls fluid into the vascular space and significantly increases urine excretion. Because of this massive fluid shift, you must monitor the patient's urine output hourly to prevent dangerous fluid imbalances or volume overload.

**50**
Which of the following interventions is a key component in the medical management of increased intracranial pressure (ICP)?
A) Administering half-strength normal saline
B) Administering plasma volume expanders
C) Administering mannitol
D) Reducing the respiratory rate on the mechanical ventilator
**Rationale:** C — When battling increased ICP, hyperosmolar therapy using agents like mannitol is essential for pulling excess fluid out of the swollen brain tissue. Interventions like hypo-osmolar fluids or reducing ventilation would actively worsen the patient's cerebral edema.

**51**

A) Alzheimer's disease
B) Brain abscess
C) Meningitis
D) Parkinson's disease
**Rationale:** C — The combination of nuchal rigidity (a stiff neck), a sudden high fever, and a severe headache makes up the classic diagnostic triad for meningitis. Recognizing these three signs together should immediately prompt you to initiate droplet precautions and prepare the patient for a lumbar puncture.

**52**
The neurological inability to produce or comprehend spoken language is clinically termed:
A) Agnosia
B) Apraxia
C) Aphasia
D) Insomnia
**Rationale:** C — Aphasia is the specific clinical term for an impaired ability to understand or produce speech following brain damage, typically from a stroke. Don't confuse this with agnosia, which is the inability to interpret sensory information, or apraxia, which is a motor execution deficit.

**53**
Which of the following diagnostic tests is the most useful for detecting and evaluating seizure activity?
A) Computed Tomography (CT) scan
B) Electroencephalogram (EEG)
C) Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
D) Skull X-ray
**Rationale:** B — An EEG uses scalp electrodes to continuously record the brain's electrical activity, making it the definitive tool for identifying the abnormal electrical discharges that cause seizures. While imaging like a CT or MRI can find structural causes like tumors, only an EEG captures the live electrical misfires.

**54**
Among the following options, which is a well-known risk factor that can directly precipitate an ischemic stroke?
A) Arteriovenous (AV) malformation
B) Cocaine use
C) Traumatic head injury
D) Rupture of a cerebral aneurysm
**Rationale:** B — Cocaine acts as an extremely potent systemic vasoconstrictor, which can severely narrow cerebral blood vessels and trigger an acute ischemic stroke. In contrast, conditions like ruptured aneurysms or AV malformations are primary causes of hemorrhagic strokes, not ischemic ones.

**55**
