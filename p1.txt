**1**
Which of the following diseases is NOT typically transmitted through contaminated food and water?
A) Hepatitis C
B) Hepatitis A
C) Cholera
D) Poliomyelitis
**Rationale:** A — Keep in mind that Hepatitis C is a bloodborne pathogen, whereas Hepatitis A, Cholera, and Polio primarily spread through the fecal-oral route via contaminated food or water.

**2**
Which concept of health is best described by the phrase "a sound mind in a sound body in a sound family in a sound environment"?
A) Ecological concept
B) Physical concept
C) Psychological concept
D) Holistic concept
**Rationale:** D — The holistic concept recognizes that true wellbeing is multidimensional, integrating the physical, mental, social, and environmental aspects of a patient's life.

**3**
When considering the epidemiological triad, which of the following groupings correctly identify the agent factors responsible for disease occurrence? (1) Biological and physical agents, (2) Mechanical and chemical agents, (3) Vertical and inoculation agents, (4) Nutrient and living agents.
A) 1, 2, and 3
B) 2, 3, and 4
C) 1, 2, and 4
D) 1, 3, and 4
**Rationale:** C — Epidemiological agent factors are broadly classified into biological, physical, chemical, mechanical, and nutritional categories. Vertical transmission and inoculation refer to the *modes of transmission*, not the causative disease agents themselves.

**Rationale:** B — Organizing and comparing problems happens later during the data analysis and diagnosis phase, not during the initial active data collection process.

**Rationale:** C — To qualify as a First Referral Unit (FRU), a facility must offer emergency obstetric care, blood transfusion services, and anesthesia. In the Indian public health system, the Community Health Center (CHC) is the primary facility equipped to meet these specific clinical criteria.

**10**
Which of the following zoonotic infections is primarily transmitted to humans through the consumption of contaminated animal milk?
A) Tuberculosis
B) Dengue
C) Plague
D) Leptospirosis
**Rationale:** A — *Mycobacterium bovis*, which causes bovine tuberculosis, can easily cross over to humans via unpasteurized milk from infected cows. In contrast, diseases like Dengue, Plague, and Leptospirosis rely on insect vectors or exposure to contaminated fluids like animal urine, not milk ingestion.

**11**
The virus responsible for causing Chikungunya belongs to which of the following viral families?
A) Flaviviridae
B) Togaviridae
C) Bunyaviridae
D) Reoviridae
**Rationale:** B — The Chikungunya virus is a member of the Togaviridae family. This is a vital epidemiological distinction to make, as similar mosquito-borne illnesses like Dengue and Yellow Fever belong to the Flaviviridae family.

**13**
How is Scabies clinically classified?
A) Non-communicable disease
B) Contagious disease
C) Bacterial infection
D) Fungal infection
**Rationale:** B — Scabies is a highly contagious parasitic infestation caused by the *Sarcoptes scabiei* itch mite. Because it spreads rapidly through direct skin-to-skin contact, it is a classic communicable condition rather than a bacterial or fungal infection.

**14**
In parasitology, the term "obligate host" refers to the:
A) Host in which the parasite remains in an asexual state
B) Temporary host used strictly for transport
C) Only host in which the parasite can complete its life cycle
D) Presence of multiple acceptable hosts for a single parasite
**Rationale:** C — An obligate host is the exclusive, definitive host required for a parasite to successfully complete its life cycle. If the parasite cannot access this specific, single host environment, it simply cannot survive and reproduce.

**15**
Which statement best describes the function of a "transport host" in the life cycle of a parasite?
A) The host where the parasite completes its sexual stage
B) The host where the parasite strictly undergoes asexual reproduction
C) The host where the organism remains alive but does not undergo developmental changes
D) The host where the life cycle inevitably terminates
**Rationale:** C — Also known as a paratenic host, a transport host acts merely as a temporary biological vehicle. The parasite survives inside this host without undergoing any growth or developmental changes until it finally reaches its definitive host.


**16**
What epidemiological term describes the constant baseline presence of an infection within a specific geographical area, without requiring any external inputs?
A) Epidemic
B) Endemic
C) Pandemic
D) Sporadic
**Rationale:** B — Endemic diseases maintain a steady baseline presence in a specific population without needing an outside source. Think of it as the "normal" expected level of a disease in that particular region, unlike an epidemic which involves a sudden, rapid spike.

**17**
In epidemiology, what is the most accurate definition of a "sporadic" disease outbreak?
A) A sudden, localized spike in disease incidence
B) An infectious disease spreading across multiple continents
C) A disease that occurs occasionally, in scattered or isolated instances
D) The constant, baseline presence of an infection in a region
**Rationale:** C — Sporadic outbreaks are those unpredictable, irregular cases that pop up randomly in isolated areas. It's helpful to remember that they lack the sustained pattern of an endemic disease or the rapid explosion of an epidemic.

**18**
Which term specifically refers to a sudden outbreak of a disease within a bird population?
A) Epidemic
B) Endemic
C) Zoonosis
D) Epornithic
**Rationale:** D — Epornithic is the avian equivalent of an epidemic in humans or an epizootic in animals. It specifically describes a rapidly spreading illness among a flock or bird population, rather than human-animal crossovers like zoonoses.

**19**
The continuous, systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health-related data required to plan and evaluate public health practices is known as:
A) Disease incidence
B) Community monitoring
C) Public health surveillance
D) Community assessment
**Rationale:** C — Surveillance is the backbone of public health, acting as an ongoing, systematic radar system to track disease trends. Don't confuse it with incidence, which simply measures new cases, or monitoring, which often lacks the rigorous, systematic approach of formal surveillance.

**20**
How is an "incubatory carrier" of an infectious disease defined?
A) An individual capable of transmitting an infectious agent to others before their own symptoms appear
B) A patient who continues shedding the disease agent during their recovery phase
C) A person who has contracted the infection but remains entirely asymptomatic throughout
D) Any human or animal simply carrying a microorganism
**Rationale:** A — An incubatory carrier sheds the pathogen and infects others specifically during the incubation period, right before they actually get sick themselves. Contrast this with convalescent carriers, who spread the illness while recovering, or asymptomatic carriers, who never show signs at all.

**21**
In the epidemiological "iceberg phenomenon" of disease, what does the submerged portion of the iceberg represent?
A) Undiagnosed and subclinical cases
B) Symptomatic clinical cases
C) Fatal cases and mortalities
D) Cases actively receiving treatment
**Rationale:** A — The vast, hidden base of the iceberg represents the massive pool of undiagnosed, asymptomatic, or subclinical carriers in the community. The visible "tip" above water only shows the clinical cases actually seeking medical care at clinics or hospitals.

**22**
What is the primary mode of transmission for whooping cough (pertussis)?
A) Direct physical contact
B) Droplet infection
C) Fomite contamination
D) Waterborne transmission
**Rationale:** B — Whooping cough is highly contagious and spreads primarily through aerosolized droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It targets the upper respiratory tract, making proximity the biggest risk factor rather than contaminated surfaces or water.

**23**
Which of the following accurately outlines the typical chain of transmission for malaria?
A) Man to Arthropod to Man
B) Man to Pig to Man
C) Mammal to Bird to Man
D) Bird to Arthropod to Man
**Rationale:** A — Malaria requires two specific hosts to complete its life cycle: humans for the sexual cycle and the female Anopheles mosquito (an arthropod) for the asexual cycle. It strictly bounces between these two, without the involvement of avian or porcine reservoirs.

**24**
By which of the following methods can an arthropod vector transmit an infection to a human host?
A) Biting and injecting saliva
B) Regurgitation of infectious material
C) Scratching infected feces into a wound
D) All of the above
**Rationale:** D — Vectors are surprisingly versatile in how they infect us; they don't just bite. Pathogens can be transmitted when a vector takes a blood meal, regurgitates stomach contents, or leaves behind feces that a host accidentally scratches into their skin.


**25**
What is the maximum size of airborne droplet nuclei capable of being inhaled deeply into the pulmonary alveoli?
A) 1 to 2 microns
B) 1 to 5 microns
C) 5 to 7 microns
D) 7 to 10 microns
**Rationale:** B — Droplets measuring 1 to 5 microns are small enough to bypass the upper respiratory defenses and settle deep into the alveolar sacs. This tiny size makes them the primary vehicle for airborne respiratory disease transmission.

**26**
Which type of immunity does a patient develop after recovering from a natural clinical infection?

B) Herd immunity

D) Specific human immunity
**Rationale:** C — When a patient naturally contracts a disease like chickenpox and mounts an immune response, their body builds natural active immunity. In contrast, artificial active immunity is achieved intentionally through vaccination.

**28**

**31**
What is the appropriate clinical action if a vial of DPT vaccine is found to have been accidentally frozen?
A) Discard the vial immediately
B) Roll it gently between the palms to warm it
C) Immerse the vial in a hot water bath
D) Leave it at room temperature to thaw
**Rationale:** A — Freezing permanently damages the adjuvants in the DPT vaccine, destroying its efficacy and drastically increasing the risk of adverse local tissue reactions. You must always discard a DPT vial if it has been exposed to freezing temperatures.

**32**
How is a "polyvalent" (or multivalent) vaccine best defined?

B) A vaccine containing two or more strains of the same microorganism
C) A vaccine containing pre-formed antibodies against a single microorganism
D) None of the above
**Rationale:** B — A polyvalent vaccine is engineered to stimulate immunity against multiple strains of the same pathogen, or even multiple different organisms, providing broader protection in a single dose. Think of the flu vaccine, which targets several circulating influenza strains at once.

**33**
What type of viral preparation is utilized in the modern anti-rabies vaccine?
A) Wild-type viruses
B) Live-attenuated viruses
C) Mixed viral strains
D) Killed/inactivated viruses
**Rationale:** D — Modern anti-rabies vaccines utilize a safely inactivated, or killed, virus to trigger an active immune response without the risk of causing the actual disease.

**34**
The presence of Negri bodies in nerve cells is a pathognomonic finding for which viral infection?
A) Rabies
B) Measles
C) Mumps
D) Pertussis
**Rationale:** A — Negri bodies are distinct, eosinophilic inclusion bodies found in the cytoplasm of infected central nervous system cells, particularly in the hippocampus. Finding them under a microscope is a definitive diagnostic hallmark of a rabies infection.

**35**

**43**

B) Rubella

**44**

**49**

**51**

B) Bacteria
C) Protozoa
D) Bacterial spores
**Rationale:** D — Simple boiling cannot reliably destroy tough bacterial spores, which act as a highly resistant resting phase designed to survive extreme environmental stress. To fully eradicate spores, you must use high-pressure steam sterilization like autoclaving.

**52**
